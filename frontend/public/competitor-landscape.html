Competitive Research

No one has perfect real-time inventory.

The better operators do not wait for a flawless system of record. They layer together live stock-out events, predicted availability, substitute logic, nearby-node rescue, and in-store guidance so customers still get a reliable experience even when upstream systems are imperfect.

Instacart Combines retailer feeds, shopper signals, shelf scans, and prediction
Target + Home Depot Treat store guidance as part of the shopping product, not a side feature
DoorDash + Amazon Build customer trust with fast stock updates and controlled substitutions

Executive Takeaway

The best benchmark is not “who has perfectly accurate inventory everywhere?” The better benchmark is “who handles uncertainty well?” The strongest setups are honest about stale signals, recover demand when the first item is missing, and route the customer toward the next best outcome.

What leaders do

  • Use multiple signals instead of relying on one legacy feed.
  • Push temporary stock changes in real time, not only in scheduled batches.
  • Offer substitutes, alternative stores, or alternate fulfillment paths before the order dies.

What they avoid

  • Pretending every in-stock flag is equally trustworthy.
  • Forcing customers to discover missing items only at checkout or by message from a shopper.
  • Waiting for a complete platform rewrite before improving reliability.

What this means for Walmart

  • The opportunity is a confidence layer, not a fantasy of instant perfection.
  • Reliability can improve meaningfully even with fragmented systems.
  • The smartest next move is layered modernization across supply chain, e-commerce, and stores.

What Competitors Are Actually Doing

Below is the clearest pattern I found from current public materials. These are not guesses about internal architecture; they are the visible operating moves companies talk about publicly.

Instacart

Hybrid inventory intelligence

Instacart is the cleanest example of a retailer-enablement company treating inventory as a living estimate. Publicly, it says it combines retailer integrations, order outcomes, shopper-collected data, and AI shelf scans to improve real-time understanding of what is actually on shelves.

  • Good lesson: do not trust one upstream feed.
  • Good lesson: use operational data from order picking as inventory signal.
  • Good lesson: use prediction to answer “likely back tomorrow morning?” not just “in stock right now?”
DoorDash

Real-time stock-out events

DoorDash explicitly pushes merchants toward real-time out-of-stock updates. Its developer docs say scheduled refreshes are not enough and provide item status webhooks so unavailable items disappear immediately instead of hours later.

  • Good lesson: temporary stock changes need their own fast event path.
  • Good lesson: batch menu refreshes are too slow for live commerce.
  • Good lesson: customer substitution preferences should travel with the order.
Amazon / Whole Foods

Trust through guardrails

Amazon and Whole Foods lean heavily on customer trust controls: explicit substitution choices, accept-or-decline flows, pickup and delivery options, and freshness guarantees for perishables.

  • Good lesson: let the customer decide where substitution is acceptable.
  • Good lesson: make “we will make it right” part of the promise.
  • Good lesson: reliability is operational and UX, not only systems architecture.
Target

Search, intent, and store-mode recovery

Target talks publicly about improving search and recommendations using availability in real time, while Store Mode helps shoppers navigate aisles and switch to alternate fulfillment if something is missing.

  • Good lesson: recovery starts in search, not only after an out-of-stock event.
  • Good lesson: store wayfinding reduces the “I know it’s somewhere here” frustration.
  • Good lesson: connect inventory, search, and fulfillment in one experience.
Home Depot

Exact store guidance

Home Depot is a strong adjacent benchmark. Its app shows local inventory and guides customers to the exact aisle and bay. That is the clearest public example of treating “find the item inside the store” as part of digital commerce, while still being honest that inventory levels cannot be guaranteed.

  • Good lesson: if you know likely location, expose it directly.
  • Good lesson: local stock counts are more useful when paired with navigation.
  • Good lesson: physical store complexity can be abstracted by the app without promising impossible precision.
Ocado / Kroger / Coles

Automation and dedicated fulfillment

Ocado’s platform is the heavy-infrastructure approach: end-to-end ecommerce, supply chain, fulfillment, and last mile. Partners like Coles emphasize better availability, fewer substitutions, and improved freshness from automated fulfillment centers and in-store fulfillment software.

  • Good lesson: dedicated fulfillment can improve order quality materially.
  • Good lesson: this is powerful but expensive and slower to scale.
  • Good lesson: this is not the only path; it is the capital-intensive path.
IKEA / Tesco

Honest fallbacks and pricing trust

IKEA publicly tells customers that stock information may not update instantly, while Tesco price-matches substitutions so customers do not feel punished when the original item is unavailable.

  • Good lesson: explicit honesty can increase trust, not reduce it.
  • Good lesson: substitution pricing needs guardrails.
  • Good lesson: when data is imperfect, the trust model matters as much as the algorithm.

The Common Pattern Across Winners

The best systems are not single-source systems. They are layered systems.

1. Real-time eventing for temporary stock-outs

Leaders create a fast lane for “this item just went out of stock” rather than waiting for a full catalog or inventory refresh.

2. Confidence, not fake certainty

They implicitly or explicitly score how trustworthy a stock signal is based on freshness, historical volatility, recent picks, or shelf observations.

3. Recovery before failure

They surface substitutes, alternate stores, pickup, later delivery, or a similar item set before the customer abandons the mission.

4. Store guidance as a product feature

The strongest in-store apps help people find the item physically, not just decide whether to buy it digitally.

5. Modernization around the edges

Most public examples suggest layering better decisioning and better signals on top of imperfect systems, not replacing everything at once.

Most important insight The market signal is clear: the better way is not “wait until all inventory is truly real time.” The better way is “treat inventory as uncertain, update it from many signals, and design the customer experience to recover gracefully.”

Best-Practice Moves Walmart Could Borrow

If the goal is pragmatic improvement without a full overhaul, these are the highest-leverage moves.

Near-term

Add a confidence layer to inventory, expose “high confidence” versus “may be stale,” support explicit substitution preferences, and rescue orders with nearby-store alternatives.

Mid-term

Capture more live stock signals from associates, pickers, and shelf scans; improve search so unavailable items roll naturally into strong substitutes or adjacent intent.

Longer-term

Build a shared inventory-confidence service across supply chain, e-commerce, and store teams so the organization stops debating whose number is “right” and starts acting on a unified confidence model.
